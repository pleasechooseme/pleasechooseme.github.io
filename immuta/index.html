PROBLEM

Policy enforcement feels automatic when it's really not.

When users create or modify policies in Immuta, these changes feel instant because there‚Äôs no clear indication in the UI of what‚Äôs happening in the background. However, the process could take hours, especially if the policy is being applied to hundreds of data sources or users.

Additionally, if a policy fails during synchronization, there's no indication of this failure in the UI, making it nearly impossible for users to pinpoint and troubleshoot errors. Instead, they must contact Customer Success, who then reaches out to Product, who diagnoses the issue.

This will allow users to quickly understand the status of their policies, take necessary actions to resolve issues, track the progress of policy changes, and identify access entitlements in the context of their data platform integration.

Primarily, users required:

PROCESS

Understanding the Product Ecosystem

I started by creating a sitemap to better understand how the entire platform worked together and how various pages interacted with each other.

While most sub-primary navigation items were housed within their corresponding primary navigation sections, represented by blue arrows, I noticed that some pages branched out to different navigation areas, indicated by pink arrows.

Noticing that many primary navigation items had their own overview pages, I decided to design one for Policies as well, given their integral role in our product.

Navigating Early Stage Design

Since it was my first time designing a dashboard, I referenced many of our existing overview pages and tweaked existing visualizations for my needs. While I wasn‚Äôt a fan of the old graphs, I struggled to devise the best way to present the following information, given the lack of clear requirements.

As I worked, I noticed that most of our existing overview pages didn‚Äôt link to other areas, despite being dashboards. Following that pattern, I initially designed without actionable elements in mind.

However, I was soon prompted to reconsider this approach when I received this feedback:

Taking a Step Back

To ensure that the dashboard was being optimized, I began mapping out user flows.

Iteration #2

In my previous iteration, I placed a failed synchronizations list at the bottom of the page. However, this required users to scroll down to see their issues rather than being able to see them immediately. I revised this by changing it to a banner at the top of the page.

While I believed this change made sense, the PMs found the logic confusing and felt it wasn't efficient enough for users.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 1

How might we enable users to quickly understand the status of their policies and take appropriate actions?

I designed a new overview page to better integrate existing parts of our platform, rather than creating new, separate pages. This overview aligns with our current pages, as shown in the site map, and provides users with a clear snapshot of their policies before exploring individual policy types.

Multipage Overview

I replaced the time frame filter on the overview page with a toggle and separated Subscription and Data Policies. In the All Policies section, I added labels for clarity to visualizations specific to only one policy.

Statistical Cards

After removing the time frame, the statistical cards now reflect the total amount of items in each category and are no longer semantically associated with the total (e.g. an increase in the total is green, misleadingly interpreted as "better").

Synchronization Status Banner

I moved the banner up to increase its prominence without being too integrated with the rest of the dashboard UI. Users can use the CTA in the banner or use the toggle for the same result.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 2

How might we provide clear and intuitive interfaces for action triggers and status indicators?

In order to address this question, I revamped our policy list view by:

  1. Completely redesigning our status column to indicate clear policy statuses and better align with a new status column design by a fellow designer
  2. Moving secondary information into a new expand / collapse column
  3. Adding different states to the Action column

Status Column

The original status column lacked states and clarity, and while the animated spinner indicated an ongoing process, it was confusing when paired with the word "ACTIVE."

I explored several iterations, using various combinations of icons and text for status flags. I was eventually inspired by the way a fellow designer used icon-less flags and instead introduced a new column with icon buttons, which provided more details on hover. This solution was optimal for my work given the range of error types that can occur when a policy fails.

01 | Error Message Popover

Following her approach, I designed four popovers to represent each error type.

02 | Expand/Collapse Column

To reduce clutter, I moved secondary information to an expand/collapse column. I initially experimented with a separate synchronization progress column but found it redundant and unnecessarily occupying valuable horizontal space.

03 | Action Column

I created various states for the action column to account for the limitations imposed on users based on the state of their policies.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 3

How might we offer detailed progress tracking and estimated completion times for policy changes?

In addition to the changes to the status column and expandable, I added a progress indicator in the policy details page.

Synchronization Progress

The progress bar informs users about the remaining synchronization time and any specific issues if they arise.

The progress bar remained consistent throughout the design process as it was immediately met with overwhelmingly positive feedback from the team.

ADDRESSING QUESTION 4

How might we seamlessly integrate access entitlement information in the language of the underlying data platform?

I added platform indicators to our data sources column to highlight the connection between data sources and data technologies. When users click on a data source, they can also see the new access entitlements column, which displays their permissions based on the technology.

These designs didn‚Äôt also change much from the beginning since they were received positively.

Platform Indicators

This displays the data platform integrations associated with the data sources targeted by each policy. Depending on the platform, users are granted different access entitlements.

01 | Access Entitlements

I added this column to work alongside the technology column, listing the actions users can take on the data source based on their granted permissions.

02 | Action Column

Similar to the policy list, I adjusted the action column to handle data source issues.

LEARNINGS

Project Takeaways

  1. Prioritize flow over fidelity: I realized that focusing on high-fidelity designs without first understanding the user flows is counterproductive. It‚Äôs crucial to take a step back and think from a high-level perspective before diving into detailed designs. This approach helps prevent misalignment and ensures a more cohesive and effective user experience.
  2. Understand the bigger picture: I also learned to consider how my work fits into the larger product ecosystem. As a result, I began to evaluate the broader impact of introducing new design patterns in my work and realized that my contributions were not isolated, but rather interconnected with the rest of the product

Internship Learnings

  1. Don't get too attached: It‚Äôs important not to get too attached to your designs. While they may look fine from a design perspective, they often require adjustments once reviewed by PMs and engineers, who provide additional insights about users, the product, and feasibility.
  2. Effective organization and documentation: Thanks to the design team, I learned the importance of annotating designs and preparing detailed presentations for stakeholder meetings. This practice not only reduces confusion but also helps identify logical gaps in the work beforehand.
  3. Frequent sharing: I discovered the value of regularly sharing my work with teammates and stakeholders, even while projects are ongoing. Keeping everyone updated helps align efforts and facilitates valuable feedback, contributing to a more cohesive and improved product.

Lastly, a huge thank you to everyone who supported me this summer‚Äîespecially Peter and the rest of the UX Design team including Kenlee, Karla, and Josh, my project collaborators, and those who made the 1.5-hour commute worthwhile.
